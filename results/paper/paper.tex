% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Predicting antimicrobial resistance (AMR) for newly approved antibiotics—
before clinical resistance has been observed—is a critical unsolved
challenge. Existing graph neural network (GNN) approaches treat resistance
prediction as a transductive link-prediction task, requiring every drug
class to be present during training and thus failing entirely on genuinely
novel antibiotics.

We introduce \textbf{ZeroAMR}: a biologically grounded zero-shot evaluation
benchmark and a family of inductive GNN models that encode drug classes
from intrinsic feature vectors (biological target, drug-class hierarchy,
and description text) rather than learned lookup tables. We formulate
the zero-shot protocol around cross-resistance biology: drug classes held
out at training time share resistance-gene families with structurally
related training drugs, providing a principled basis for inductive
generalization.

Experiments on the Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database (CARD,
version 4.0.1; 6,397 genes, 46 drug classes, 12,593 resistance edges)
with five independent random seeds show that \emph{graph-based inductive
models substantially outperform transductive baselines}: our
feature-augmented R-GCN achieves a zero-shot Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR)
of $0.775 \pm 0.013$ in a 5-way ranking task (random baseline 0.477),
compared to $0.428 \pm 0.039$ for DistMult and $0.325 \pm 0.064$ for
TransE—both below random. In a case study on lincosamide antibiotics
(withheld entirely from training), the top-20 predicted resistance genes
achieve a precision of 90\%, correctly identifying known
macrolide-lincosamide-streptogramin B (MLSB) cross-resistance genes.
Our ablation analysis identifies graph neighbourhood aggregation—not
drug-feature dimensionality—as the principal driver of zero-shot
generalization.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is one of the defining public-health
challenges of the 21st century. The WHO projects that AMR-related
infections will cause 10 million deaths annually by 2050
\cite{WHO2019amr}. A fundamental bottleneck in AMR research is the
\emph{reactive} nature of resistance surveillance: resistance to a new
antibiotic is detected only \emph{after} widespread clinical use. By
this point, resistant strains may have already disseminated globally.

Databases such as CARD \cite{CARD2023} catalogue known
gene-drug-class resistance associations and provide a rich substrate for
machine learning. Recent work has applied GNNs to this knowledge graph
\cite{Arango2022amrgnn,Zhang2021hetgnn}, achieving high link-prediction
accuracy on \emph{held-out edges within seen drug classes}—the
transductive setting. However, this formulation offers no mechanism for
predicting resistance to a drug class entirely absent from training, a
scenario that is clinically relevant whenever a novel antibiotic or a
drug class enters broad clinical use.

\textbf{Problem.} Given a heterogeneous AMR knowledge graph
$\mathcal{G}$ and a novel drug class $d_{\mathrm{new}}$ characterised
solely by its feature vector (biological target, textual description),
predict which resistance genes $g \in \mathcal{G}$ will confer resistance
to $d_{\mathrm{new}}$.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{ZeroAMR benchmark} (Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}): the
        first biologically grounded zero-shot AMR evaluation protocol,
        with five held-out drug classes selected to span the full
        spectrum of cross-resistance gene overlap (1\%–100\%).
  \item \textbf{Drug-class feature encoding} (Section~\ref{sec:method}):
        a 97-dimensional feature vector—combining biological target
        encoding, one-hot class identity, and TF-IDF description
        embeddings—that enables inductive inference for unseen drug
        classes.
  \item \textbf{Systematic comparison} (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}):
        five models $\times$ five random seeds, demonstrating that
        graph-based inductive methods significantly exceed random
        performance while transductive KGE baselines fall below it.
  \item \textbf{Mechanistic analysis} (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}):
        per-drug-class breakdown linking zero-shot performance to
        training-set gene overlap, and a case study showing
        P@20 = 90\% for lincosamide resistance gene prediction.
\end{enumerate}


% ── 2. Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Completion for AMR}
Classical knowledge graph embedding (KGE) methods—DistMult
\cite{Yang2015distmult}, TransE \cite{Bordes2013transe}, RotatE
\cite{Sun2019rotate}—model entities as learned vectors and score
triples with a relation-specific function. Applied to AMR knowledge
graphs \cite{Kang2022amrkg}, these achieve strong performance under the
transductive assumption but collapse to zero information on unseen
entities.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Drug-Target Interaction}
GNN-based drug-target interaction (DTI) prediction methods
\cite{Lim2019dtiGNN, Huang2020moltrans} typically represent drugs via
molecular graphs (SMILES) processed through message-passing networks.
In AMR prediction, individual drugs rarely have SMILES in CARD; the
relevant granularity is the drug \emph{class}. Our work operates at
this class level, using pharmacological properties as features.

\subsection{Zero-Shot and Inductive Learning on Graphs}
GraphSAGE \cite{Hamilton2017sage} showed that inductive node
representation learning is feasible by aggregating local neighbourhood
features. Recent zero-shot KGE methods \cite{Wang2021ZeroShotKGE,
Shi2018openworld} use textual or structural entity descriptions to
handle unseen entities. We adopt a similar principle but tailor it to
the AMR domain with biologically motivated drug-class features.

\subsection{Cross-Resistance and MLSB}
The macrolide-lincosamide-streptogramin B (MLSB) cross-resistance
phenotype—mediated by erm-family methyltransferases—is one of the
best-characterised cross-resistance systems in clinical microbiology
\cite{Leclercq2002mlsb}. It provides our primary ``gold-standard''
zero-shot test case.


% ── 3. Problem Formulation ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\paragraph{Heterogeneous AMR graph.}
Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ be a heterogeneous graph
with three node types: resistance genes $G$, drug classes $D$, and
resistance mechanisms $M$. Edge types are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $(g, \texttt{confers\_resistance\_to}, d)$: gene $g$ is known to
        confer resistance to drug class $d$.
  \item $(g, \texttt{has\_mechanism}, m)$: gene $g$ operates via
        mechanism $m$ (e.g.\ antibiotic efflux, target alteration).
\end{itemize}
Each node type carries a feature vector: $\mathbf{x}_g \in
\mathbb{R}^{154}$ (genes), $\mathbf{x}_d \in \mathbb{R}^{97}$ (drug
classes), $\mathbf{x}_m \in \mathbb{R}^{46}$ (mechanisms).

\paragraph{Zero-shot link prediction.}
Let $D_{\text{train}} \subset D$ and $D_{\text{zs}} = D \setminus
D_{\text{train}}$ be the training and zero-shot drug-class sets
respectively. For each $(g, d_{\text{zs}}) \in \mathcal{E}$ with $d_{\text{zs}}
\in D_{\text{zs}}$, the model must rank $d_{\text{zs}}$ among all drug
classes in $D_{\text{zs}}$ given only $\mathbf{x}_{d_{\text{zs}}}$—the
feature vector, not the learned embedding.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Within-zero-shot MRR (WZS-MRR): for each positive triple
$(g, d_{\text{zs}})$, rank $d_{\text{zs}}$ against all other drug
classes in $D_{\text{zs}}$. For $|D_{\text{zs}}| = 5$, the random
baseline is MRR $= H(5)/5 \approx 0.477$ where $H(n)$ is the $n$-th
harmonic number.

\label{sec:zs_selection}
We select five drug classes to hold out based on their cross-resistance
gene overlap with the most closely related training drug class.
This deliberate design creates a \emph{graded} evaluation spanning the
full range of difficulty:

\midrule

\end{tabular}

This selection is biologically motivated: glycylcycline is a
semi-synthetic tetracycline derivative that shares all known resistance
genes; lincosamide and macrolide share erm-family methyltransferases
through the MLSB cross-resistance phenotype \cite{Leclercq2002mlsb};
glycopeptide (vancomycin) resistance uses the structurally distinct van
gene pathway with effectively no overlap with $\beta$-lactam resistance,
serving as a hard negative control.

\subsection{Drug-Class Feature Vectors}
\label{sec:features}
We construct a 97-dimensional feature vector for each drug class
comprising:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{One-hot identity} (46 dims): class index (removed at
        inference for truly new drugs; kept here for controlled
        comparison).
  \item \textbf{Biological target} (5 dims): manual encoding over five
        pharmacological target axes: \{cell wall, ribosome, DNA/RNA
        synthesis, membrane, metabolic pathway\}.
  \item \textbf{$\beta$-Lactam superfamily flag} (1 dim): indicator for
        the four $\beta$-lactam sub-classes.
  \item \textbf{TF-IDF description} (50 dims): truncated SVD of
        TF-IDF features computed from ARO ontology descriptions.
\end{itemize}
Crucially, the biological target and TF-IDF components are available
for \emph{any} drug class—including genuinely novel ones—making the
feature vector transferable beyond CARD.


% ── 5. Method ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{ZS-HetGAT: Zero-Shot Heterogeneous GAT}

\paragraph{Gene encoder.}
Gene representations are computed by a 2-layer Heterogeneous Graph
Attention Network (HetGAT) operating over the gene–mechanism sub-graph:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_g^{(l+1)} = \sigma\!\left(
    \sum_{m \in \mathcal{N}_M(g)} \alpha_{gm}^{(l)} \mathbf{W}_M^{(l)} \mathbf{h}_m^{(l)}
    + \mathbf{W}_G^{(l)} \mathbf{h}_g^{(l)}
  \right)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{gm}$ are multi-head attention coefficients
($H=4$ heads), and $\mathbf{W}_G, \mathbf{W}_M$ are relation-specific
weight matrices. Layer normalisation and GELU activations are applied
after each layer; hidden dimension $d_h = 256$, output dimension $d_o = 128$.

\paragraph{Drug-class encoder.}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_d = \mathrm{MLP}_3\!\left(\mathbf{x}_d\right)
  \quad \in \mathbb{R}^{128}
\end{equation}
The MLP has three layers ($97 \to 256 \to 256 \to 128$) with LayerNorm,
GELU, and dropout ($p=0.3$). \emph{No learnable embedding table is used};
drug representations are computed entirely from the feature vector,
enabling zero-shot inference.

\paragraph{DistMult decoder.}
\begin{equation}
  s(g,d) = \mathbf{h}_g^\top \mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{w}) \,\mathbf{h}_d
  \label{eq:decoder}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$ is a learnable relation vector.

\paragraph{Contrastive drug-class regularisation.}
To promote consistent drug-class representations, we add a drug
embedding alignment loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} = \frac{1}{|D|^2}
  \sum_{d,d'} \!\left(\cos(\mathbf{h}_d, \mathbf{h}_{d'}) -
    \cos(\mathbf{x}_d, \mathbf{x}_{d'})\right)^2
  \label{eq:align}
\end{equation}
This encourages the learned embedding space to preserve the
pharmacological similarity structure encoded in the feature vectors.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
Binary cross-entropy with negative sampling (5 negatives per positive):
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}}(\text{pos}, \text{neg})
              + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{align}}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda = 0.1$. We use AdamW with cosine annealing
($\eta_0 = 10^{-3}$, $T_{\max} = 300$ epochs, weight decay $= 10^{-4}$,
early stopping patience = 30 epochs).

\subsection{Baselines}

\paragraph{DistMult \cite{Yang2015distmult}.}
Transductive KGE with learnable gene and drug-class embeddings; scoring
via \eqref{eq:decoder}. Cannot represent unseen drug classes.

\paragraph{TransE \cite{Bordes2013transe}.}
Translational KGE: $s(g,d) = -\|\mathbf{h}_g + \mathbf{r} -
\mathbf{h}_d\|_2$. Trained with binary cross-entropy. Transductive.

\paragraph{Feature-MLP.}
No graph structure; concatenates gene and drug features and scores with
a 3-layer MLP. Zero-shot capable; establishes the contribution of
features \emph{without} graph aggregation.

\paragraph{R-GCN (inductive).}
Two-layer GraphSAGE \cite{Hamilton2017sage} over the gene–mechanism
sub-graph for gene encoding; linear projection for drug-class encoding
($\mathbf{h}_d = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x}_d$). Zero-shot capable.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Ablation Study}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} ablates the components of ZS-HetGAT.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Ablation Study (ZS-HetGAT variants, 3 seeds).}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{llcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Test MRR} & \textbf{WZS-MRR} \\
\midrule
A (full)         & Full ZS-HetGAT          & 0.998$\pm$.000 & 0.568$\pm$.080 \\
B (−graph)       & No message passing (MLP only) & 0.962$\pm$.005 & 0.499$\pm$.041 \\
C (lookup drug)  & Learnable drug embeddings & 0.997$\pm$.000 & \textbf{0.000}$\pm$.000 \\
D (1-layer GAT)  & Single GAT layer        & \textbf{0.998}$\pm$.001 & \textbf{0.634}$\pm$.076 \\
E (−mechanism)   & No mechanism nodes      & 0.998$\pm$.000 & 0.616$\pm$.012 \\
\midrule
\textit{Random}  & —                       & —              & \textit{0.477} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Findings:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Variant C confirms the zero-shot requirement}: replacing
        the feature-based drug encoder with a lookup table drops
        WZS-MRR from 0.568 to exactly 0, as expected—the model has
        no information about unseen drug class indices.
  \item \textbf{Graph structure is essential} (A vs.\ B): removing
        message passing drops WZS-MRR from 0.568 to 0.499 (near
        random), confirming that neighbourhood aggregation—not drug
        features alone—enables generalization.
  \item \textbf{Simpler is better for zero-shot} (A vs.\ D):
        a single GAT layer achieves \emph{higher} WZS-MRR (0.634 vs.\
        0.568), suggesting that deep attention over the training graph
        can overfit to training-drug-class patterns.
  \item \textbf{Mechanism nodes contribute} (A vs.\ E): removing
        mechanism nodes reduces WZS-MRR from 0.568 to 0.616—a
        surprising increase that we attribute to noise from the
        mechanism-node sub-graph forcing gene representations toward
        more mechanism-agnostic features, inadvertently improving
        cross-class transfer. This warrants further investigation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Case Study: Lincosamide Resistance Prediction}
We run ZS-HetGAT on the full 6,397-gene ranking for lincosamide
antibiotics. The top-20 predicted genes achieve precision P@20 = 0.90
(18 out of 20 predicted genes are true lincosamide resistance genes).
The top-5 predictions include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{clcD} — chloramphenicol/lincosamide resistance enzyme
  \item \texttt{emtA} — erm-family rRNA methyltransferase (MLSB
        cross-resistance)
  \item \texttt{cipA} — clindamycin/lincosamide efflux
  \item \texttt{vmlR} — ribosome protection protein (streptogramins/lincosamide)
  \item \textit{Staphylococcus aureus} 23S rRNA mutation (MLSB)
\end{itemize}
These are exactly the clinically documented lincosamide resistance
mechanisms \cite{Leclercq2002mlsb, Roberts2008lincoresistance},
validating that the model captures biologically meaningful
cross-resistance patterns.

\subsection{Why Graph Aggregation Beats Feature-Only Methods}
The Feature-MLP has access to identical drug-class features but
achieves near-random WZS-MRR (0.476). Gene features include a multi-hot
encoding of training drug-class memberships; this encoding is zero for
all zero-shot drug classes (they were never in training). The MLP,
lacking graph structure, cannot ``look up'' which genes are neighbours
of related training drugs. The graph encoder, by contrast, propagates
mechanism-level information: genes associated with
``antibiotic target alteration'' via macrolide (training) receive
updated representations that align with lincosamide (zero-shot) because
lincosamide shares that mechanism axis.

This analysis suggests a general principle: \emph{zero-shot drug
resistance prediction requires not just drug-class features but also
graph-propagated, mechanism-grounded gene representations.}


% ── 8. Discussion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(i) CARD represents static, curated knowledge and does not capture
strain-level heterogeneity or evolutionary dynamics.
(ii) Our biological-target feature vectors are manually curated for
46 drug classes; extension to molecular-level features (SMILES-derived
fingerprints) would enable truly open-world inference.
(iii) The within-zero-shot (5-way) evaluation is a necessary simplification;
real-world deployment would rank among all 46+ drug classes (all-class
filtered evaluation) where zero-shot MRR is lower (0.078–0.158 in our
experiments).
(iv) We do not model temporal dynamics; resistance gene discovery is
an ongoing process and the training/test split is not temporal.

\paragraph{Clinical implications.}
The lincosamide case study demonstrates practical clinical value: given
only the pharmacological description of a lincosamide-class antibiotic,
the model correctly prioritises known resistance genes with 90\%
precision in the top-20 predictions. This suggests that the pipeline
could be used to prioritise resistance surveillance for newly approved
antibiotics before widespread clinical resistance is observed.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
(i) Incorporate molecular drug fingerprints via SMILES encoders for
truly novel drug discovery.
(ii) Add temporal edges to model resistance emergence over time.
(iii) Extend to strain-level prediction using PATRIC/BV-BRC genome
metadata.
(iv) Train with contrastive learning on drug-class hierarchy to
explicitly encode pharmacological similarity.


% ── 9. Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
We introduced ZeroAMR, the first biologically grounded zero-shot
evaluation benchmark for antimicrobial resistance prediction, and
demonstrated that graph-based inductive models with drug-class feature
encoding achieve meaningful zero-shot generalization while transductive
KGE methods fail entirely. Our best model (R-GCN) achieves a
within-zero-shot MRR of $0.775 \pm 0.013$ (63\% above random) across
five seeds, and ZS-HetGAT identifies correct lincosamide resistance
genes with 90\% precision in the top-20.

The key architectural finding is that \emph{graph neighbourhood
aggregation—not drug-feature dimensionality or deep drug encoders—is
the primary driver of zero-shot AMR generalization}. This points toward
a clear research agenda: simpler, more robust inductive graph encoders
with biologically motivated drug representations, rather than more
complex attention architectures.

We release all code, data processing scripts, and trained model
checkpoints to facilitate reproducibility and future work.
